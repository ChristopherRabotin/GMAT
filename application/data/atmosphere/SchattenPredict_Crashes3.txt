Of particular note in the current prediction is that
in later years, for cycle 25 and 26, are the later times
for solar cycle peak. This is based on many displays of evidence
that the Sun's solar cycle is slowing down. Firstly this current
solar cycle is behaving much more slowly, than previous cycles.
This is common with low cycles, and has been known for a half a
century. Additionally, the toroidal flows on the Sun's disk
have been slowing down. Also the polar fields reversed very slowly.
Hence our future cycles have been adjusted to the rate, that
is likely to be, at present. The timing uncertainties have also been 
raised.

In the table below, after the month and year columns, the next column (labeled mean) provides the 
predicted monthly mean smoothed estimate of future solar radio flux. This attempts 
an extension into the future of the PENTICTON 10.7 CM FLUX solar radio telescope data 
tuned to 10.7 cm wavelength (published by NOAA in their solar-geophysical data bulletins); 
following this, we provide the estimated + 2 sigma and - 2 sigma error bounds on these MEAN fluxes.
 THE SIGMAS DO NOT REPRESENT VARIATIONS ASSOCIATED WITH THE DAILY/MONTHLY/YEARLY VARIATIONS IN 
ACTIVITY, BUT RATHER THE ESTIMATE OF OUR UNCERTAINTY IN THE PREDICTED AVERAGE BEHAVIOR. 
Monthly variations add additional noise to the uncertainties we provide. The sixth column 
provides a rough measure of a weighted planetary geomagnetic index, ap. This index 
shows a large natural variability associated with geomagnetic storms which the user 
should be aware of. Subsequent columns are repeats of these four columns, for "early" 
and "late" cycles, based upon an uncertainty in the timing of predicted cycles, since the 
"timing uncertainty" is different from the amplitude uncertainty in predicting future solar
behavior. The author bears no responsibility for the resultant use or misuse of the predicted solar data.


We have seen reversals of both polar fields; this was an atypical cycle, in having a
long slow reversal. This would portend a gradual slowing down of activity.
This behavior would suggest that we are over the hump in activity. Nevertheless,
the sun always seems to throw some curve balls our way. Small solar Cycles still have
large swings in the amounts of activity that the Sun displays.
Nevertheless, we can expect a secular decline after this maximum phase declines. This has 
manifested itself in a very real way with the recent remarkable rise in activity just a couple of months ago.
Namely, in February of this year, 2014, the smoothed F10.7 Radio Flux reached a value of 
smoothed peak 170.3 SFU (solar flux units). Currently, now, this is the peak value for solar cycle 
#24, as it surpassed the previous peak 2 years ago! Likely, we shall now seek solar activity decrease
on average, till solar minimum, in roughly the year, 2020.The reason is that low cycles
have long, slow declines. If there are any questions about this, or any other aspect of
this prediction, please e-mail the author at ken.schatten@ai-solutions.com.
